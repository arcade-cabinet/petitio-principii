# Lore

Petitio Principii takes place inside a single argument. The player is navigating it the way one navigates a dream — rooms are rhetorical spaces, corridors are inferences, and the whole thing eventually folds back on itself.

## Setting

The argument is not held by any character. There is no narrator. The text addresses the player directly, and the player's commands become part of the argument's trajectory — questioning it, accepting it, rejecting it, or tracing it back.

The setting is a **memory palace at night**. Each room is a rhetorical claim, architected as a space: the Premise Hall, the Conclusion Balcony, the Analogical Corridor. Starlight filters through (that's the Phaser backdrop). The teletype terminal is the player's instrument of engagement — the one concrete object in an otherwise abstract space.

## The circle

The argument is, inevitably, circular. Every seed produces a graph where at least one conclusion, chased far enough, returns to its own premise. This is the petitio principii. Completing the circle (by reaching a `circular` or `meta` room and typing `accept`) is the implicit win condition — though the game never tells the player they "won" in those terms. The argument merely notes: *"You have completed the circle. The argument was always about itself."*

## Voices

There are no characters, but there are voices:

- **The argument** — the implicit narrator. Addresses the player in second person. Tone: patient, dignified, faintly amused, occasionally pompous.
- **Voices that respond** — when the player uses `ask`, something answers. Always evasive, always referring back to an unjustified premise.
- **The player** — only speaks through commands, which appear in the teletype output prefixed with `>`.

## The terms are real; the content is absurd

The rhetorical vocabulary (petitio principii, ad hominem, straw man, equivocation, false dichotomy, slippery slope, appeal to authority) is academically correct. The *content* of any individual argument is procedurally generated surrealism — melting watches, lobster telephones, things dissolving into logic. The point is not to teach fallacies. The point is to make the player feel the shape of an argument that goes nowhere.

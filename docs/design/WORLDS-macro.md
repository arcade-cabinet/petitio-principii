# Worlds — Macro Design

> Two arguments are going to be braided into one walk. This document defines
> what each argument *is* — its place, its voice, its temperament, its
> ending — before any room is drawn or any connection is wired. Later layers
> (meso, micro) are only as good as this one.

The game already has a conceit (`docs/LORE.md`): the player is a mind
re-entering an argument it once made. What has been missing is a **place**
for that argument to live — somewhere the mind can walk. T109 answers the
question by providing two such places, hand-authored, each a complete
argument in itself, braided at playtime by seed. This document is the
philosophical foundation for the two.

Nothing here specifies rooms. Rooms arrive in `WORLDS-micro.md`. Nothing
here specifies regions or density curves. Those arrive in `WORLDS-meso.md`.
What arrives **here** is identity — the thing that, if wrong, makes every
later decision wrong.

---

## 1. Why two worlds

One world is a location. Two worlds is an **argument**.

The game's thesis is that the shape of reasoning is circular — that any
sufficient argument, walked far enough, arrives at its own premise. A
single hand-authored world can gesture at that thesis but cannot *enact*
it, because a single world has one aesthetic, and the player learns its
aesthetic, and the circularity starts to feel like a stylistic tic. The
reading collapses: "oh, this author just likes writing rooms that curve
back." The fallacy becomes a *mannerism*.

Two worlds, braided, rescue the mechanic. When the circle closes across
worlds — when the Premise Hall in the Subterranean Library and the
Premise Hall in the Celestial Court say *the same thing in opposing
dictions* — the player experiences the circularity as **structural, not
authorial**. The argument is the same argument whether it wears a
scholar's robes or a courtier's silk. That is the point of the game; two
worlds is how we say it.

The pairing also gives us the **voice audit** — the core quality gate for
the authoring work. If a tester, shown ten rooms blind, can tell World A
apart from World B ≥ 90% of the time, the worlds have genuine identities.
If they cannot, we have written one world twice.

## 2. The two worlds

### World A — *The Subterranean Library*

**One sentence.** A mind's argument as a descent through a private
archive no one else has ever read, where the authority of each claim is
the fact that you once wrote it down.

**Element.** Below. Earth, stone, paper. Candles gutter. Air thickens.
Sound is small and close. Light is the color of old vellum.

**Posture.** You are **descending**. Each room is a little further under.
The floor tilts imperceptibly toward the circular room at the bottom,
though nothing about the tilt is ever stated — it is felt in how verbs of
motion read. *Step down. Pass under. Lean in.* Never *rise, cross, above*.

**Authority figure.** A **Librarian** who is nowhere visible but
everywhere implied: card catalogs in your own hand, marginalia that
correct your reasoning gently, a stamp on the verso of every page
reading *READ ONCE, 18—*. You gradually realise the Librarian is a past
version of you, one who has already finished the argument and is leaving
you notes. This is never stated. It is a voice property.

**Moral temperature.** Complicit. The library does not accuse you; it
colludes with you. Its fallacies feel like favors.

**End-state (when the walk ends in A).** *The Reading Room*, where a
single volume lies open at the page you began on. The inscription is the
premise, now in firmer type. You understand you have been turning the
pages of a book you once wrote, in a library you once built, to confirm
a conclusion you once reached. Accepting is closing the book. Rejecting
is closing the book slightly more slowly.

**One-word mood.** *Admittance.*

### World B — *The Celestial Court*

**One sentence.** A mind's argument as a public ascent through a
tribunal of brilliant rooms, where the authority of each claim is the
consensus of figures who were never there.

**Element.** Above. Air, crystal, glass. Chandeliers burn clean. Sound
is long, marbled, echoing. Light is cold and pale, the color of a winter
noon through high windows.

**Posture.** You are **ascending**. Each room is a little higher. The
geometry opens — galleries widen, atria lift, observatories pierce
ceilings. Verbs of motion read *rise, cross, above, mount, emerge*.

**Authority figure.** A **Court** — never seen, only inferred: the clap
of a gavel you didn't see fall, the rustle of robes behind a screen, a
voice in the first-person plural: *"we find it so."* Gradually you
realise the Court is also a past version of you, rendered in the
grammar of committee. This is never stated. It is a voice property.

**Moral temperature.** Ceremonious. The Court does not collude with you;
it **confirms** you. Its fallacies feel like verdicts.

**End-state (when the walk ends in B).** *The Inner Observatory*, a
telescope trained not at the sky but at the floor. Through the eyepiece
you see yourself in the lower chambers, bearing the premise upward,
delivering it here. Accepting is closing the shutter. Rejecting is
leaving the shutter ajar for the next ascent.

**One-word mood.** *Verdict.*

### Why *this* pair

The two worlds are not aesthetic opposites for the sake of contrast. They
are the **two natural postures of self-justification**:

1. **The private version** — *"I have convinced myself."* World A. An
   argument with no audience but you, conducted in the archive of your
   own past reasoning. This is where conviction is *remembered into
   existence*.
2. **The public version** — *"we find it so."* World B. An argument
   performed for an imagined tribunal whose approval ratifies it. This
   is where conviction is *decreed into existence*.

Every human self-justification is some mixture of the two. World A and
World B are that mixture pulled apart. When the braid re-mixes them per
seed, each play is a different blend of archived and decreed, private
and public — which is what the argument *felt* like, on the night you
first had it.

The premise is the same premise. The fallacy is the same fallacy. Only
the **diction of surrender** differs.

## 3. Voice guidelines

The two voices must pass a **blind-read test**: given a paragraph of
prose with no room title, a reader familiar with the game can identify
which world it is from ≥ 90% of the time. This section is the reference
for authoring.

### 3.1 Shared rules (both worlds)

- **Second person, always.** The mind narrates to itself.
- **No NPCs with names.** Voices are always *your own voice in another
  register* (see `VOICE.md`). The Librarian and the Court are both
  *forms of you*.
- **No fourth-wall breaks.** Never say *the argument*, never say
  *the game*, never address the player directly.
- **Rhetorical vocabulary is real.** Premise, syllogism, enthymeme,
  analogy, petitio principii — used correctly. The content those terms
  describe may be Dadaist; the terminology is not.
- **Descriptions under 80 words.** A breath, not prose.

### 3.2 World A — The Subterranean Library

| Dimension | Tendency |
|---|---|
| Syntax | Short declaratives. Comma splices rare. Full stops early. |
| Register | Quiet, scholarly, intimate. A hand on your sleeve. |
| Tense | Present, but slipping into the pluperfect for authority: *You had read this before.* |
| Pronouns | Strongly *I / my / your / our* — possessive, private. |
| Verbs | Of handling: *turn, lift, stamp, shelve, read, underline, leave-open.* |
| Sound imagery | Small, enclosed. *Paper creases. A candle gutters. Your sleeve catches a page.* |
| Temperature words | *Warm, close, stale, amber, underlined, foxed, damp.* |
| Forbidden | Ceremony nouns (*tribunal, court, bench, gallery, panel*). Plural authority (*we, the court*). Fanfare verbs (*proclaim, decree, ratify*). Glass imagery. Height imagery. |
| Signature move | A margin note, in your hand, corrects the page you are reading. |
| Signature image | *Old paper. Candle. A hand you recognize as your own.* |

Example cadence (diagnostic — not for any specific room):

> You step down into a low alcove. A ledger is open on the reading
> stand, your handwriting in the left column, a gentler version of your
> handwriting in the right. The right-column argues the left into
> stillness. You read both, and agree with the second, and move on.

### 3.3 World B — The Celestial Court

| Dimension | Tendency |
|---|---|
| Syntax | Longer periodic sentences. Subordinate clauses that resolve formally. Semicolons permitted. |
| Register | Public, performed, slightly archaic. A voice across a hall. |
| Tense | Present, but lifting into the future perfect for authority: *It will have been found so.* |
| Pronouns | The third-person-ceremonial and first-person-plural: *the court, we, the assembled.* |
| Verbs | Of pronouncement: *rise, cross, rule, find, ratify, enter (a finding), strike, seal.* |
| Sound imagery | Long, reverberant. *A gavel falls somewhere. Robes rustle. A chord holds in the vault.* |
| Temperature words | *Cold, pale, high, crystalline, silver, clear, tall, drawn.* |
| Forbidden | Intimacy nouns (*alcove, carrel, marginalia, ledger, candle, hand*). Singular-possessive authority. Paper imagery. Depth imagery. |
| Signature move | A finding is read aloud by no one and becomes settled law. |
| Signature image | *Cold glass. Vaulted air. A robe retreating behind a screen.* |

Example cadence:

> You rise into a pale atrium whose ceiling is lost in geometry. A
> finding has been read, though by whom is unclear, and the finding is
> that the premise you brought with you was always a finding. The
> assembled do not disagree. You cross the floor.

### 3.4 The blind-read test

A paragraph should fail the audit if, stripping the title, a careful
reader cannot tell A from B. Common failure modes to watch for during
authoring:

- **Shared neutral verbs.** *You enter. You see. You notice.* These
  belong to neither voice. Replace with a voice-specific verb (*step
  down* / *rise into*).
- **Aesthetic leakage.** An A room reaching for *vaulted* because it
  wanted grandeur. A B room reaching for *amber* because it wanted
  warmth. Each world denies itself the other's palette.
- **Authority drift.** A B room uses *I remember*; an A room uses *we
  find*. The pronouns are load-bearing.
- **Mood mis-match.** An A room with **fanfare**; a B room with
  **confession**. A is private admittance; B is public verdict. Every
  beat should honor that.

## 4. Thematic pairing — what the braid is doing

A seed draws a ~30-room walk that weaves between A and B at connection
nodes. Three things must be true of every braid for the pairing to work:

1. **The same argument is being made in both dictions.** Whichever
   rooms the seed visits, the player is always walking *one* argument,
   in two languages. The premise, the fallacy, the circle — these are
   the argument's fingerprints, and they must be recognizable in either
   world.

2. **A crossing is never neutral.** When the walk hops from A to B (or
   back), the player should feel the *diction* shift — the verbs, the
   air, the light. Crossings are **voice-events**, not just node
   transitions. Hops early in the walk feel like broadening; hops late
   feel like collusion across a tribunal (the private argument
   recruiting the public one to confirm it, or vice versa).

3. **The circle closes in whichever world the walk ends in.** Both
   world-specific end-states (A's Reading Room, B's Inner Observatory)
   are self-sufficient closings. But each is a **different reading** of
   the same ending. A's closing is *quiet admission*; B's closing is
   *formal finding*. Same petitio principii; different diction of
   surrender. A determined seed can end in either; the weaver chooses
   per the PRNG walk.

### Why the player should want the braid (not one world twice)

Across replays, the player's memory of the game is **not of one world
they know well**, but of a single *argument they have walked through
under different weather*. The Premise Hall they remember is a composite
— sometimes amber, sometimes crystal, but always the same premise on
its pedestal. This is closer to how memory actually works: the content
is stable, the sensorium shifts. The game argues (quietly, without
saying so) that the **argument is the invariant**; the diction is the
variable. Which is the game's thesis.

One world, replayed, would feel familiar. Two worlds, braided, feel
*real* — because real arguments also come back to you in different
weather.

## 5. End-state — when the walk is over

Every braided walk terminates in a **closing chamber** hand-authored in
whichever world the PRNG leaves the player in. There are exactly two
such chambers per seed's possible outcome:

- **A's closing — The Reading Room.** A low alcove, a single open
  volume, the inscription is the premise now in firmer type. The
  Librarian is not there, though a chair is pulled out and the candle
  is freshly trimmed. `ACCEPT` closes the book softly. `REJECT` closes
  the book slightly more slowly. Either way the book closes. The
  closing narration acknowledges the seeded path behind it — *you came
  by the Fallacy Cellar, by the Antechamber of Definitions that
  defined themselves; you arrived here anyway.*

- **B's closing — The Inner Observatory.** A pale dome; a telescope
  inverted, looking down. Through the eyepiece you see yourself in the
  chambers below, carrying the premise upward. The Court is not
  present, though a gavel lies on the bench, warm. `ACCEPT` closes the
  shutter; the closing is declared from the gallery by a voice that
  leaves no name. `REJECT` leaves the shutter ajar and the next ascent
  begins at the foot of the stair the next time. Either way the finding
  has been entered. The closing narration acknowledges the seeded
  ascent — *you passed the Atrium of Findings, you crossed the Crystal
  Gallery; you emerged here anyway.*

### The game's rule about endings

Neither ending is "better." Neither is a success. The circle closes in
either world because **the circle is not a property of the world — it
is a property of the argument**. The player's felt sense, either way,
should be: *I arrived here. I was always going to arrive here. The way
in was the way through.*

The only **false ending** is a seeded walk that reaches a cap on turns
without ever entering a closing chamber — this is the
*indefinite loop* failure mode (Act III without closure). The meso
layer must ensure every seed has a ≥ 95% chance of reaching one of the
two closing chambers within 40 rooms. Budgeting happens there.

## 6. What this macro layer does not answer

These are deliberate deferrals, by layer:

**Meso (`WORLDS-meso.md`).**
- How many regions each world has and what their names are.
- Where the connection-nodes sit structurally and how the braid traverses them.
- Which puzzle groups (T110) live in which world, which span both, and roughly how many rooms each claims.
- Where the gnomes (T108) range and which of them are A-resident, B-resident, or A/B-bridging.
- The pacing density curve across the ~40 rooms of each world.
- How we guarantee reachability of a closing chamber inside the turn budget.

**Micro (`WORLDS-micro.md`).**
- Individual room titles, bodies, verb-responses, and exits.
- The exact JSON / TS shape each authored room takes.
- 3–4 sample rooms per world that define the authoring style.
- The Zod schema sketch that will validate authored rooms at build time.

**Schema/loader, authoring, weaver.** Engineering, not design; live
outside these three docs.

## 7. The standing commitments

If we do nothing else right, we do these:

1. The two worlds sound like they were written by **two different
   people** — voice first, features second.
2. Every authored room passes the **Zork test**: can you describe it
   to a friend in a way that makes them want to visit it?
3. The **braid is a voice-event**, not a technical detail — a crossing
   is felt in the prose the moment it happens.
4. Every seed **closes** — somewhere in the Reading Room or somewhere
   in the Inner Observatory, the argument admits itself.
5. The two worlds, taken together, argue that **the argument is the
   invariant** and the diction is the variable. That thesis is the
   pairing's reason for existing.

Once these five commitments hold on the page, the meso and micro layers
can begin.
